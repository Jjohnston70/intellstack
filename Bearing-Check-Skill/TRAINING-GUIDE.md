## Overview

This training guide teaches you to use the Bearing Check framework for validating decisions under uncertainty. After completing this guide, you'll be able to:

- Run a Full Bearing Check on major decisions
- Run a Quick Bearing Check for moderate decisions
- Recognize when to use Bearing Check
- Avoid common decision-making errors
- Document decisions for future learning

**Estimated Training Time:** 2-3 hours
**Prerequisites:** None (familiarity with TNDS skills helpful)

## Module 1: Understanding the Framework

### 1.1 What is Bearing Check?

A **bearing check** is what navigators do before committing to a course. They verify their position against multiple fixed reference points. If the references don't agree, they don't move until they understand why.

The Bearing Check framework applies this concept to decisions:
- Verify the decision against 8 different reference points (checkpoints)
- If checkpoints don't agree, investigate before committing
- Build in staged approach and failure planning

### 1.2 The Problem It Solves

**The Typical Decision Pipeline (Fragile):**
```
See success story → Find a pattern → Copy the approach → Go all-in → Hope it works
```

**The Bearing Check Pipeline (Robust):**
```
See outcomes → Rebuild full dataset → Test explanations → Model uncertainty →
Check constraints → Act in stages → Plan for failure → Update with evidence
```

### 1.3 The Seven Cognitive Traps

Bearing Check guards against these common errors:

| Trap | What It Is | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Survivorship Bias** | Only seeing winners | "All successful founders work 80 hours" (ignoring failed founders who also worked 80 hours) |
| **Winner's Curse** | Overpaying to win | Bidding too high because you're excited about an opportunity |
| **Gambling Bias** | Doubling down on losing | "I've invested too much to quit now" |
| **Wrong Variable** | Optimizing the wrong thing | Copying a successful person's morning routine instead of their actual skills |
| **Ignoring Uncertainty** | Overconfidence | "This will definitely work" |
| **External Validity** | Assuming transferability | "It worked for them, so it'll work for me" |
| **Non-Stationarity** | Assuming stable conditions | "The market will stay this way" |

## Module 2: The Eight Checkpoints

### 2.1 Checkpoint Overview

| # | Checkpoint | Core Question | Time |
|---|------------|---------------|------|
| 1 | Ground Truth | How often does this succeed? | 10-15 min |
| 2 | Graveyard Recon | Who failed doing this? | 10-15 min |
| 3 | Mechanism Check | WHY does this work? | 10-15 min |
| 4 | Fixed Points | Which factors are durable? | 5-10 min |
| 5 | Odds Assessment | What are realistic odds? | 10-15 min |
| 6 | Constraint Fit | Does this fit MY constraints? | 10-15 min |
| 7 | Staged Advance | How can I test before committing? | 5-10 min |
| 8 | Pre-Mortem | If this fails, what went wrong? | 10-15 min |

### 2.2 Deep Dive: Each Checkpoint

#### Checkpoint 1: Ground Truth

**Purpose:** Establish the base rate before stories cloud judgment.

**What to Do:**
1. Ask: "How often does [this type of decision] succeed?"
2. Search for population-level statistics
3. Find data for situations similar to yours
4. Accept the base rate, don't explain it away

**Example:**
- Decision: "Should I start a consulting business?"
- Ground Truth: ~50% of consulting businesses survive 5 years; solo consultants have higher survival but lower growth

**Common Mistake:** "My situation is different" — Usually it isn't.

---

#### Checkpoint 2: Graveyard Recon

**Purpose:** Study failures, not just successes.

**What to Do:**
1. Actively search for failure stories
2. Look for post-mortems, "lessons learned"
3. Ask: "What patterns appear in failures?"
4. Identify what differentiated winners from losers

**Example:**
- Decision: "Should I launch a SaaS product?"
- Graveyard Recon: Common failure patterns include building before validating demand, running out of runway, underestimating marketing effort

**Common Mistake:** Dismissing failures as "they did it wrong."

---

#### Checkpoint 3: Mechanism Check

**Purpose:** Understand WHY something works, not just THAT it works.

**What to Do:**
1. Separate visible behavior from underlying mechanism
2. Ask: "What forces actually produce this outcome?"
3. Generate alternative explanations
4. Test: Does explanation hold up against evidence?

**Example:**
- Observation: "Successful people wake up at 5am"
- Wrong conclusion: Wake up early → success
- Mechanism check: Successful people have control over schedule; early rising is effect, not cause

**Common Mistake:** Copying behavior instead of understanding mechanism.

---

#### Checkpoint 4: Fixed Points

**Purpose:** Identify which success factors are durable vs. timing-dependent.

**What to Do:**
1. List success factors
2. Ask for each: "Would this still matter in 5 years?"
3. Identify what you control vs. what you don't
4. Assess timing/trend dependency

**Durable Factors (Fixed Points):**
- Distribution/reach
- Trust/reputation
- Skill depth
- Feedback speed
- Network quality

**Example:**
- Decision: "Should I invest in cryptocurrency?"
- Fixed Points: Few (highly timing-dependent, outside your control)
- Compare to: "Should I invest in building a skill?" — More fixed points

---

#### Checkpoint 5: Odds Assessment

**Purpose:** Replace certainty with probability ranges.

**What to Do:**
1. Estimate probability as a RANGE (not a point)
2. Define what success looks like
3. Define what failure looks like (multiple scenarios)
4. Ask: "What happens if I'm wrong?"

**Example:**
- Decision: "Should I pursue this client?"
- Odds Assessment: 30-50% close rate for this client type; if I'm wrong, I've invested 5 hours; if I'm right, $5K project

**Common Mistake:** Speaking in certainties instead of probabilities.

---

#### Checkpoint 6: Constraint Fit

**Purpose:** Verify the strategy fits YOUR constraints.

**What to Do:**
1. List your constraints (time, money, emotional bandwidth, etc.)
2. List what the strategy requires
3. Compare and identify mismatches
4. If mismatch: adapt strategy or abandon

**Constraints to Check:**
- Time horizon
- Capital available
- Emotional temperament
- Risk tolerance
- Lifestyle constraints
- Opportunity cost

**Example:**
- Strategy requires: 18 months of low income
- Your constraint: Need income within 6 months
- Mismatch: Strategy doesn't fit, need to adapt or abandon

---

#### Checkpoint 7: Staged Advance

**Purpose:** Design a path that starts small and scales with evidence.

**What to Do:**
1. Define Stage 1: Minimum viable test
2. Define success signals (what proves it's working)
3. Define abort signals (what makes you stop)
4. Plan decision points

**Stage Design Template:**
```
Stage 1: [Small test] - [Investment] - [Duration]
  Success signal: [X]
  Abort signal: [Y]

Stage 2: [Increased commitment if Stage 1 works]
...
```

**Example:**
- Decision: "Should I offer a new service?"
- Stage 1: Offer to 3 existing clients at reduced rate
- Success signal: 2+ accept and deliver successfully
- Abort signal: 0 accept or delivery fails

---

#### Checkpoint 8: Pre-Mortem

**Purpose:** Imagine failure BEFORE it happens.

**What to Do:**
1. Write the failure story in past tense
2. Be specific about what went wrong
3. Assess survivability (financial, emotional, professional)
4. Check if strategy is repeatable

**Pre-Mortem Template:**
> "It's [date one year from now]. The decision to [X] failed. Here's what happened: [specific failure narrative]. The signs I ignored were: [warning signs]. The impact was: [consequences]."

**Example:**
> "It's January 2027. The decision to launch restaurant-command failed. We spent 6 months building something that didn't fit restaurant workflows. Our first client churned after 3 months. Meanwhile, we lost two field service clients because we were distracted."

## Module 3: Running a Bearing Check

### 3.1 Full Bearing Check (60-90 minutes)

**When to Use:** Major decisions with significant downside

**Process:**
1. **Scope the Decision (5-10 min)**
   - State the decision clearly
   - Identify stakes
   - Confirm Full check is appropriate

2. **Run All 8 Checkpoints (70-100 min)**
   - Complete each checkpoint
   - Document findings
   - Assign flag level (Green/Yellow/Red)

3. **Synthesize Final Bearing (5-10 min)**
   - Count flags
   - Make recommendation (GO/CONDITIONAL/NO-GO/DEFER)
   - Define next action

### 3.2 Quick Bearing Check (20-30 minutes)

**When to Use:** Moderate decisions, familiar territory, time pressure

**Process:**
1. **Scope the Decision (5 min)**
2. **Run 3 Checkpoints (20 min)**
   - Checkpoint 1: Ground Truth
   - Checkpoint 5: Odds Assessment
   - Checkpoint 8: Pre-Mortem
3. **Quick Bearing (5 min)**
   - Make recommendation
   - Define next action

### 3.3 Single Checkpoint

**When to Use:** User asks about specific aspect

**Map user questions to checkpoints:**
- "What's the base rate?" → Checkpoint 1
- "Why do these fail?" → Checkpoint 2
- "Why does this work?" → Checkpoint 3
- "What are the odds?" → Checkpoint 5
- "Do I have what it takes?" → Checkpoint 6
- "What could go wrong?" → Checkpoint 8

### 3.4 When to Stop Early

Stop the Bearing Check if:
- 3+ Red flags appear
- Ground Truth + Graveyard Recon both Red
- Constraint mismatch is fundamental

Don't waste time analyzing a bad decision.

## Module 4: Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: Identify the Trap

For each scenario, identify which cognitive trap is at play:

1. "I've been working on this project for 6 months. I can't quit now."
   - Trap: _______

2. "Every successful entrepreneur I've read about wakes up at 5am."
   - Trap: _______

3. "The market has been going up for 5 years. It'll keep going."
   - Trap: _______

4. "I'm 90% sure this will work."
   - Trap: _______

**Answers:** 1) Gambling Bias, 2) Survivorship Bias + Wrong Variable, 3) Non-Stationarity, 4) Ignoring Uncertainty

---

### Exercise 2: Quick Bearing Check Practice

Run a Quick Bearing Check on this decision:

**Decision:** Should I attend a $500 industry conference next month?

Complete:
1. **Ground Truth:** What's the success rate of networking at conferences?
2. **Odds Assessment:** What are realistic odds I'll get value from this?
3. **Pre-Mortem:** If I go and it's a waste, what happened?

Then make a recommendation: GO / CONDITIONAL GO / NO-GO / DEFER

---

### Exercise 3: Mechanism Check Practice

For this observation, separate behavior from mechanism:

**Observation:** "Successful consultants all have strong LinkedIn presences."

1. What's the visible behavior?
2. What's the underlying mechanism?
3. What alternative explanations exist?
4. What should you actually do?

---

### Exercise 4: Full Bearing Check Practice

Run a Full Bearing Check on this decision:

**Decision:** Should I offer a productized service (fixed scope, fixed price) instead of custom consulting?

Complete all 8 checkpoints, then provide:
- Final recommendation
- Confidence level
- Next action

## Quick Reference Card

### The 8 Checkpoints

1. **Ground Truth** — Base rate for this decision type
2. **Graveyard Recon** — Who failed and why
3. **Mechanism Check** — WHY it works
4. **Fixed Points** — Durable vs. situational factors
5. **Odds Assessment** — Probability ranges
6. **Constraint Fit** — Matches MY constraints?
7. **Staged Advance** — Test before committing
8. **Pre-Mortem** — Imagine failure now

### Flag Interpretation

- 🟢 Green: Checkpoint passed
- 🟡 Yellow: Concern but manageable
- 🔴 Red: Serious issue

### Decision Rules

- 0-1 Red flags: Usually GO
- 2 Red flags: CONDITIONAL GO
- 3+ Red flags: NO-GO or DEFER

### Quick Bearing Check

Use checkpoints 1, 5, and 8 only.
