# The Bearing Check: Detailed Checkpoint Reference

## Checkpoint 1: Ground Truth

### What It Means
Before listening to any success story, establish the baseline probability of success for people in similar situations. This grounds expectations in reality rather than outlier narratives.

### Key Questions
- What percentage of people who attempt this actually succeed?
- What's the success rate for people with my specific background/resources?
- How is "success" defined in these statistics?

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "I want to quit my job and start a SaaS company like [successful founder]"

**Ground truth check:**
- ~90% of startups fail within 10 years
- ~75% of venture-backed startups fail
- For bootstrapped SaaS: ~20% reach $10K MRR within 2 years
- For first-time founders without technical background: success rate drops further

**Impact:** This doesn't mean "don't try." It means size your bet appropriately and have a runway plan.

### Common Mistakes
- Using success stories as your denominator instead of all attempts
- Ignoring selection effects in the data you're seeing
- Comparing yourself to outliers rather than the median outcome

## Checkpoint 2: Graveyard Recon

### What It Means
Actively seek out people who did the same thing and failed. Understand what differentiated failures from successes.

### Key Questions
- Who else tried this approach and failed?
- What did the failures have in common?
- What warning signs did failures miss?
- Am I seeing survivorship bias in my information sources?

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "I should invest in crypto because my friend made 10x"

**Graveyard recon:**
- Find people who invested at the same time and lost
- Identify what your friend did differently (timing, risk management, luck)
- Calculate: for every 10x winner, how many 50%+ losers?

**Impact:** Reveals whether success was skill or timing/luck. Informs position sizing.

### Common Mistakes
- Only reading success stories and case studies
- Dismissing failures as "they didn't execute well enough"
- Assuming you're immune to the same failure modes

## Checkpoint 3: Mechanism Check

### What It Means
Visible behaviors (wake up at 5am, use this tool, follow this routine) are not the same as causal mechanisms. Many "winner behaviors" are common behaviors that don't predict success.

### Key Questions
- Would this strategy still work if the hidden factors were different?
- What are alternative explanations for this outcome?
- Does the "cause" survive when I consider confounders?
- Is this correlation or causation?

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "Successful CEOs wake up at 5am, so I should too"

**Mechanism analysis:**
- Possible mechanisms: more focused time, signaling discipline, specific morning tasks
- Confounders: successful CEOs also have support staff, don't commute, control their schedules
- Alternative explanation: people with high energy levels both wake early AND succeed
- Test: Would waking at 5am help if you work better at night and get less sleep?

**Impact:** Adapt the underlying principle (protected focus time) rather than copying the surface behavior.

### The Rule
A good explanation must survive contact with alternative explanations. If your "cause" collapses when you consider confounders, it's not a cause. It's a story.

## Checkpoint 4: Fixed Points

### What It Means
Some success factors are durable across time and contexts. Others are situational, trendy, or depend on specific circumstances that may not repeat.

### Stable Factors (prioritize these)
- Distribution/reach
- Trust/reputation
- Skill depth
- Feedback speed
- Iteration count
- Network quality
- Risk control

### Flashy/Unstable Factors (discount these)
- Current trends/hype
- Specific platform algorithms
- Timing-dependent arbitrage
- Celebrity endorsements
- Viral moments

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "This influencer grew to 1M followers using this content strategy"

**Fixed points analysis:**
- Stable: understanding audience psychology, consistent output, genuine expertise
- Unstable: specific algorithm timing, trend-riding, platform features that changed
- Question: Would this work if started today? Would it work on a different platform?

**Impact:** Extract the stable principles, discard the situational tactics.

## Checkpoint 5: Odds Assessment

### What It Means
Replace binary thinking ("this works/doesn't work") with probability ranges. Understand the distribution of outcomes, not just the expected value.

### Key Questions
- What are the odds this works?
- What are the odds under different scenarios?
- What's the range of possible outcomes (best case, worst case, most likely)?
- How confident should I be in my probability estimates?

### Probability Reframes
Instead of "this works" say:
- "This increases odds by approximately X%"
- "This works under these conditions"
- "This fails in these scenarios"
- "Success rate is roughly X% with high/medium/low confidence"

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "Should I get an MBA to advance my career?"

**Odds assessment:**
- Probability of salary increase: ~70% (varies by school tier, industry)
- Range of increase: 20-100% (wide distribution)
- Probability of career pivot success: ~40-60%
- Downside: 2 years + $150K opportunity cost
- Scenarios where it fails: wrong industry fit, recession during graduation, could have advanced without it

**Impact:** Frame decision as "given these probabilities and my risk tolerance, is this expected value positive for me specifically?"

## Checkpoint 6: Constraint Fit

### What It Means
Even if a strategy works on average, it may require conditions you don't have. External validity (what works in general) doesn't guarantee it works for you.

### Constraints to Check
- **Time horizon:** Can you wait for results?
- **Emotional temperament:** Can you handle the volatility?
- **Capital size:** Do you have enough to survive failures?
- **Access to information:** Do you have the same information advantages?
- **Lifestyle constraints:** Does this fit your life situation?
- **Risk tolerance:** Can you afford to lose this?
- **Opportunity cost:** What are you giving up?

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "Warren Buffett holds stocks for decades, so I should too"

**Constraint fit check:**
- Time horizon: Buffett has effectively infinite horizon; you may need money in 10 years
- Capital: He can survive 50% drawdowns; can you?
- Information: He has access to company management; you don't
- Temperament: He's famously unflappable; are you?

**Impact:** Adapt the strategy to your constraints. Don't cosplay someone else's situation.

### The Rule
Copying a strategy without copying its constraints is like copying a champion's training plan without having their body, coach, or recovery capacity. Same surface. Different system. Different outcome.

## Checkpoint 7: Staged Advance

### What It Means
Don't go all-in on any single decision. Start small, test, gather feedback, increase exposure only when evidence accumulates. Keep a margin of safety.

### The Staged Approach
1. Start small
2. Test
3. Gather feedback
4. Increase exposure when evidence accumulates
5. Keep a margin of safety

### Reinforcement Learning Mindset
You don't make one perfect decision. You run a policy: act -> observe -> update -> act again.

### For Big Branching Decisions
Think in Monte Carlo Tree Search terms: Try "cheap simulations" of different paths before committing deeply. Prototype before you build the factory.

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "I want to become a freelance consultant"

**Staged advance:**
- Stage 1: Take one client while employed (test demand, pricing)
- Stage 2: Build to 3 clients, validate repeatability
- Stage 3: Reduce employment to part-time if possible
- Stage 4: Go full-time only when monthly income covers 6+ months expenses
- Maintain: Keep emergency fund, don't burn bridges

**Impact:** You learn real market feedback before taking irreversible actions.

### The Principle
All-in is not confidence. It's fragility. The goal isn't to be right once. The goal is to avoid ruin while compounding learning.

## Checkpoint 8: Pre-Mortem

### What It Means
Before you act, imagine you fail. Work backwards from failure to understand vulnerabilities and whether you can survive the downside.

### Pre-Mortem Questions
- What exactly caused the failure?
- Can I survive that failure?
- Would I still keep executing the plan after a win or a loss?
- Is the strategy repeatable, or is it a one-time gamble?

### The Repeatability Test
If you can't repeat it, it's not a strategy. It's a gamble.

### Example Application

**Scenario:** "I'm going to invest my savings in this opportunity"

**Pre-mortem:**
- Imagine it fails completely. What happened?
  - Market turned, timing was wrong, information was incomplete, fraud
- Can I survive that?
  - Do I still have income? Emergency fund? Can I recover in 2-5 years?
- Would I do this again after a loss?
  - If no, I'm gambling, not investing
- Is this repeatable?
  - Can I make similar bets over time, or is this a once-in-a-lifetime shot?

**Impact:** If failure is unsurvivable or the strategy isn't repeatable, it's not a strategy worth pursuing.
